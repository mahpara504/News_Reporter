WORLD BANK AND ASIAN DEVELOPMENT BANK FACING CRITICISM OVER MUTUAL RELIANCE FRAMEWORK

2 January 2025

                    The World Bank and the Asian Development Bank (ADB) have come under significant scrutiny following their introduction of the Full Mutual Reliance Framework (FMRF), a collaborative plan aimed at streamlining their processes for co-financed projects. The framework, designed to allow one institution to take the lead in managing a project’s operational policies and procedures, seeks to reduce the administrative burdens and delays typically involved in the coordination between the two organizations. By allowing one bank to lead the financial and operational elements of a project, the FMRF aims to accelerate project implementation and make the process more efficient, ultimately benefiting borrowing countries across Asia and the Pacific, which are often in need of quick and effective solutions for infrastructure, education, and health development.

                    However, this initiative has raised considerable concerns among non-governmental organizations (NGOs), civil society groups, and development experts, who have voiced strong criticism of the framework. Critics argue that the mutual reliance arrangement may inadvertently undermine the environmental and social safeguards that are essential to ensuring that development projects do not harm local communities or the environment. The primary concern is that by allowing one institution to assume responsibility for the due diligence process of the entire project, both the World Bank and ADB could lower their standards of oversight and accountability. There is worry that the FMRF will dilute the rigorous checks and balances that are intended to ensure that projects are thoroughly assessed for their potential social and environmental impact.
                    
                    The critics argue that this could result in weaker oversight, which may lead to harmful environmental or social consequences in countries that rely heavily on international financial support. They emphasize that the institutions’ obligation to protect vulnerable populations and the environment cannot be outsourced or delegated. Furthermore, critics contend that the FMRF could undermine the ADB’s own Environmental and Social Framework (ESF), which is designed to safeguard the interests of affected communities and maintain high standards for environmental protection. By bypassing some of the standard procedures for environmental and social risk assessment, the FMRF might create loopholes that could allow harmful projects to go ahead with less scrutiny.
                    
                    In response to these concerns, several NGOs and advocacy groups have called for the withdrawal of the FMRF, arguing that it compromises the integrity of development finance practices and undermines public trust in the institutions. They have suggested that the framework, in its current form, is incompatible with the existing safeguards and mechanisms in place at both the World Bank and the ADB. Some groups have also called on both institutions to reconsider the framework and ensure that any future cooperation between the two organizations upholds environmental and social protection standards. They argue that prioritizing efficiency should not come at the expense of ensuring that development projects are sustainable, equitable, and aligned with the long-term well-being of local communities.
                    
                    Despite these criticisms, both the World Bank and the ADB continue to defend the Full Mutual Reliance Framework. They argue that the FMRF is necessary to enhance operational efficiency, reduce transaction costs, and speed up project implementation. By cutting down on duplicative efforts and streamlining administrative processes, both institutions contend that the FMRF will help them better serve their client countries, particularly those in need of urgent infrastructure and development projects. The institutions also maintain that the framework does not alter their commitment to environmental and social safeguards, and that any concerns regarding the quality and accountability of projects will be addressed through improved coordination between the two organizations.
